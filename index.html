The Glorious Brain

The human brain is the most complex structure in the universe.
There are 100 billion neurons present in the brain
Yet, even with technological advancements, little is known about our brain.

What we do know however, are the specialized regions in our brain for different function. For example, the cerebrum (also known as the cortex) is the largest part of the human brain.
It comprises of four lobes; the temporal, occipital, frontal and parietal lobe
Temporal lobe is associated with audition, memory, and speech
Occipital lobe is associated with visual processing
Frontal lobe is associated with higher order executive functions such as reasoning, working memory and problem solving
Parietal lobe is associated with the integration of sensory information and other functions such as language processing

The Human Connectome Project

The Human Connectome Project is one of the current projects that aims at mapping the brain connectivity and localizing their functional aspects.
Researchers from the University of Southern California seek to navigate the brain by mapping the different brain pathways, the various circuitry and the cells in specific regions of the brain
By doing so, it allow comparison of living brains, therefore, better understanding of underlying brain mechanism that could be potentially utilize to treat diseases of the brain

Motor And Sensory Homunculus

A cortical homunculus is a distorted representation of the human body, based on a neurological "map" of the areas and proportions of the brain dedicated to processing motor functions or sensory functions, for different parts of the body.(Wiki)
Therefore, based on these homunculus, there are more areas of the sensory and motor cortex dedicated to your fingers.
That's why paper cuts hurts so badly!

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

  • Chronic neurodegenerative condition causing muscle wasting, paralysis and death usually within 3 to 5 years due to respiratory failure
  • Amyotrophy and scarring of cortico-spinal tracts (one of the main motor pathways)
  • Examples:
    • Motor Neurone Disease (MND)
    • Lou Gehrig's disease
    • Charcot's disease
    • ALS
  • Epidemiology:
    • New cases per annum: ~2/100,000
    • Males are more affected than females
  • Life time risk (UK):
    • Males: 1 in 350
    • Female: 1 in 472

  • Symptoms:
    • Muscle atrophy and spasticity
    • Progressive denervation and secondary muscle weakness of limbs, trunk, tongue and respiratory intercostal muscles
    • Impaired swallowing and speech
    • Spastic weakness and paralysis affecting almost all skeletal muscle
    • Respiratory failure
    • No impairment of bladder, bowel or sexual function
    • Oculomotor, sensory and autonomic function spared
    • Cognitive function may be affected in a minority of cases (~15%)
  • How it differs from other motor neurons diseases
    • Presence of both upper and lower motor neurone signs
    • Lower motor neurone (LMN): Muscle weakness, wasting, fasciculations, cramps
    • Upper motor neurone (UMN): Stiffness and slowness of movement, slow and clumsy speech
  • Treatments
    • Most treatments aims at reducing the symptoms of ALS
    • Speech and swallowing: Speech therapist
    • Mobility around home: Occupational therapist
    • Swallowing and feeding: Nasogastric tube or PEG (percutaneous endoscopic gastronomy feeding tube into the stomach)
    • Breathing: Oxygen, assisted ventilation (face mask)
    • Symptomatic treatments: Cramps/muscle spasm
